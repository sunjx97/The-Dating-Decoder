I remember the exact moment my first real heartbreak hit. I was standing in my kitchen, staring at a half-empty coffee cup, and suddenly it felt like the world had lost its color. The person I thought I’d spend years with was gone. And I had no idea how to be me without them.

That was years ago. Since then, I’ve had my heart bruised a few more times (haven’t we all?), and I’ve watched friends navigate their own wreckage. Slowly, I started noticing patterns—things that helped, and things that made it worse.

If you’re in the middle of your own breakup right now, I’m not here to tell you it’s easy. But I am here to share what I’ve learned. Not from textbooks—from real life.

---

## 1. You Have to Feel It to Heal It

In those first days, every instinct screams at you to numb the pain. Drink. Binge-watch. Swipe through dating apps just to feel wanted. Anything to escape the ache.

But here’s the thing: **pain that isn’t felt doesn’t disappear. It just waits.**

I tried the distraction route. It worked for about a week. Then I woke up at 3 a.m. with the same crushing weight on my chest, and I realized I’d only been postponing the inevitable.

What actually helped? Sitting with it. Crying when I needed to. Writing angry letters I never sent. Letting myself be sad without calling it weakness.

**Try this:** Give yourself 10 minutes a day to feel everything—no distractions, no music, no phone. Let the sadness come. It will pass through you, not stay forever.

---

## 2. Don’t Do It Alone (Even If You Want To)

I’m an introvert. When I’m hurting, my first instinct is to crawl into a cave and emerge weeks later, fully healed. But that’s not how humans work.

The friends who showed up with takeout and didn’t try to fix me—they were my lifeline. The ones who just sat on the couch with me while I cried? Heroes.

You don’t have to tell everyone everything. But pick one or two people who can hold space for you without judgment. Let them in.

**And please:** If you’re struggling to eat, sleep, or function for more than a few weeks, reach out to a therapist. There’s zero shame in needing professional help. Breakups can trigger real depression—don’t tough it out alone.

---

## 3. Cut the Cord (Yes, Including Social Media)

This one is brutal but necessary. You cannot heal while you’re still peeking into their life.

I know. You want to know if they’re hurting too. You check their Instagram at 2 a.m. and see them smiling with friends, and suddenly you feel ten times worse. It’s a form of self-harm, really.

**Block. Mute. Unfollow.** Do whatever it takes to stop the digital bleeding. You don’t need to know what they’re doing. Their healing journey is separate from yours.

If you have things at their place, ask a friend to retrieve them. If you work together, set boundaries. Make it possible to truly disconnect.

---

## 4. Rediscover Who You Were Before Them

When you’re in a relationship, you become a “we.” You share habits, jokes, routines. And when that person leaves, you can feel like half of yourself is missing.

But here’s the truth: **you were a whole person before they showed up.** That person is still inside you, maybe a little dusty, but intact.

After my worst breakup, I made a list of things I loved doing that my ex didn’t enjoy. Hiking at dawn. Watching terrible reality TV. Painting ugly watercolors. I started doing them again, alone at first, then with friends. Slowly, I remembered who I was when no one was watching.

**Ask yourself:** What did I love before this relationship? What have I stopped doing? Start there.

---

## 5. Trust That Time Actually Does Something

I know. Everyone says “time heals” and you want to punch them. But it’s not that time itself does the healing—it’s what you do with it.

Time gives you distance. It lets the obsessive thoughts fade. It shows you, slowly, that the world didn’t end. You wake up one morning and realize you didn’t think about them for a whole hour. Then a whole morning. Then a whole day.

It’s not linear. You’ll have setbacks. But if you keep doing the work—feeling, connecting, rediscovering—time becomes your ally instead of your enemy.

---

## One Last Thing

Healing isn’t about becoming “over it.” It’s about integrating the loss into your story without letting it define you. You’ll love again. Maybe differently, maybe more wisely, but you will.

And the person who comes out the other side? They’re stronger. More compassionate. More alive.

You’ve got this. One day at a time.
